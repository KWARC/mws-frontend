68.2-2 PadicNumber
‣ PadicNumber( fam, rat )( operation )
‣ PadicNumber( purefam, list )( operation )
‣ PadicNumber( extfam, list )( operation )

(see also PadicNumber (68.1-2)).

PadicNumber creates a p 𝑝 p -adic number in the p 𝑝 p -adic numbers family fam. The first form returns the p 𝑝 p -adic number corresponding to the rational rat.

The second form takes a pure p 𝑝 p -adic numbers family purefam and a list list of length two, and returns the number p 𝑝 p ^list[1] * list[2]. It must be guaranteed that no entry of list[2] is divisible by the prime p 𝑝 p . (Otherwise precision will get lost.)

The third form creates a number in the family extfam of a p 𝑝 p -adic extension. The second argument must be a list list of length two such that list[2] is the list of coefficients w.r.t. the basis { 1 , … , x f - 1 ⋅ y e - 1 } 1 … ⋅ superscript 𝑥 𝑓 1 superscript 𝑦 𝑒 1 \{1,\ldots,x^{{f-1}}\cdot y^{{e-1}}\} of the extended p 𝑝 p -adic field and list[1] is a common p 𝑝 p -part of all these coefficients.

p 𝑝 p -adic numbers admit the usual field operations.

gap> efam:=PadicExtensionNumberFamily(3, 5, [1,1,1], [1,1]);;
gap> PadicNumber(efam,7/9);
padic(120(3),0(3))

A word of warning:

Depending on the actual representation of quotients, precision may seem to "vanish". For example in PadicExtensionNumberFamily(3, 5, [1,1,1], [1,1]) the number (1.2000, 0.1210)(3) can be represented as [ 0, [ 1.2000, 0.1210 ] ] or as [ -1, [ 12.000, 1.2100 ] ] (here the coefficients have to be multiplied by p - 1 superscript 𝑝 1 p^{{-1}} ).

So there may be a number (1.2, 2.2)(3) which seems to have only two digits of precision instead of the declared 5. But internally the number is stored as [ -3, [ 0.0012, 0.0022 ] ] and so has in fact maximum precision.
